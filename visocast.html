VisoCast

Project Overview

Duration: 2 months

Tools: Paper, dry-erase marker, Adobe Photoshop, Adobe Illustrator, Adobe InDesign, Adobe Acrobat, AxureRP

Deliverables: UX Research, UX Brief (Strategy, Functional Specs, etc.), Comparative Analysis, Card Sort, Sitemap, Lo-fi Wireframes

My Own Role: "UX Designer / UX Researcher", Visual Designer

Time to Read: 10 minutes


Introduction

I did the UX design for a hypothetical Netflix and YouTube combination (and competitor) called VisoCast for credit in my UX Design Certificate at George Brown College. A few of the aspects of the design that I did are represented below.

(P.S. the layout of this page differs from the others: it isn't numbered; and it isn't a typical project flow, because it wasn't a typical project; these were course assignments. I start with a card sort, move into wireframes for mobile, and then selections from a strategy document.)

Card Sort

A card sort helps to develop a product's information architecture; you sort facets of the app into categories of best fit, which can inform your product's navs, or menus.

Card sorts require people to test assumptions with; e.g. where would a user expect to see an "Account Options" button: within a category called "Settings", or a menu called "Profile"?

The answer to that question might seem obvious to you; but a review of almost any website or app will reveal odd information architecture decisions made, perhaps, without any user involvement by a designer or developer based on what made the most sense to them, as individuals, rather than a consideration for who would be using their product the most.

I conducted what's called as a "closed sort" for VisoCast. A closed sort is where the categories are determined beforehand, and the participants fit the various pages into whichever category they think fits it best (as opposed to an open sort, which is where participants develop the categories themselves).

Sitemap

A sitemap is a hierarchical map that illustrates the structure of a product. They're taxonomical, showing where (and how) a product's content is grouped, hopefully within conventions that users would expect.

I developed the VisoCast sitemap before moving on to the wireframes.

The sitemap is not 100% reflective of my card sort; I completed it after the card sort had been graded by my instructor, and areas for improvement within the information architecture (I got good marks, though; I promise!).

Wireframes

A wireframe, one of the most common artifacts of UX design, is a guide for a website’s (or app’s, etc.) framework.

It’s an illustration of how elements will be presented to the user in different contexts (e.g. for a website: how the elements are laid out on mobile vs. on a desktop monitor, etc.)

A wireframe enables designers to develop (and test) the information architecture of a design, which can illustrate where improvements should be made and, because no development capital has been spent, improvements aren’t difficult to implement.

I used a combination of AxureRP and Adobe Illustrator to develop these annotated wireframes for VisoCast's (hypothetical) mobile app.

(P.S. the wireframes are from a larger document; and feature .pdf page controls, which are non-functional here.)



Splash Screen

I don't live in the future - despite all of the hours that I spent in that stupid time machine - so apps still need time to load. It's important to communicate load times (or status) to users; here, I included a load wheel with a clockwise animation (clockwise animations suggest forward temporal movement).



Log In Screen

I wanted the log in to be an obvious process; that is, clear at each step what the user should do (or has failed to do). I used validation on the username and password fields to prompt user to correct errors; and the placeholder text in the password field assists the user to recall the password format.



Sign Up Screen

I wanted the user's sign up to be short, with validation (of email address, username, etc.) throughout to prompt user to change details before registration attempt. The text beneath the screen title switches to a "Forgot Username/Password?" link on enter of an email address (or username) already associated with an account. I opted to use labels for the text fields (vs. placeholder text) because unlike log ins, sign up forms don't have conventional flows, and I wanted the user to be reminded of their current field (without an exit press). I included a "lock" graphic at the top of the screen to suggest to the user that the personal information entered was secure.



Recover Password or Username Screen

An email address and instructions to recover the user's account information (with a link to further support if the user needs it).



Home Screen

I modeled the home screen after Netflix's home screen: a main content tile (for promoted or popular content) with secondary tiles genrefied beneath. VisoCast wasn't a real project; and I didn't need to reinvent the real wheel.

As a UX Designer, awareness of which conventions users expect from a service is important. Users have become comfortable with Netflix's interface and will expect something that's at least similar from a competing service (technology being equal, that is; there aren't established conventions yet for, e.g., virtual reality, wearable screen devices, etc.). The one key difference was the inclusion of the "Upload" button, where users could upload their own recorded content to the service to be shared with other users.



Main Menu

I wanted the menu to focus first on VisoCast's content; and I included a number of selectable filters. The user can also enter the User Profile screen (shown in 2.1.2) or log out.



Filtered Content Screen

I opted again for convention; but with the inclusion of a "Random" button for each of the content categories to enable users who are game to watch any content right now to do just that.

Huge content volumes - like Netflix has - can present users with a "paralysis of choice" (is that a phrase? I just write stuff a lot of the time); a randomize option might ameliorate that feeling a little bit.



User Profile Screen

I wanted a simple profile with an emphasis on the user's content: either their own uploaded content, or public playlists. I wanted to encourage VisoCast's social aspect where appropriate.



Search Entry Screen

Simple search, with simple instructions. The users would start to see results after ~3 characters entered.



Search Results Screen

Again, I opted for convention; but with the inclusion of the "Random" button for each list. I needed to include user uploaded content and premium content within the same search. To determine where each would be dilineated within the search, deeper filtering, etc. would've required user research that was outside of the scope of the project.



Upload Menu

I wanted to encourage user uploads (as ownership over content within a system fosters repeated user engagement and community), so I included a large "Record Now" button at the bottom of the upload menu.



View My Uploads Screen

I wanted users to have a good overview of the content that had been uploaded, and immediately know - via buttons - actions related to the content.



Upload New Screen

The user is shown content available to be uploaded, with frames within each item to give the user a more obvious picture of what each item is (for error prevention).



Submit Upload Screen

I wanted the users to have an obvious picture of what sort of functions could be performed on their upload - edits, tagging, etc. - before the final submission. For certain users, posting content online is a personal - and therefore, intimidating - experience; for those users, offering a measure of control over their upload can ease their fear (and encourage content submission from more users, in general).



Media Player

For the player, I wanted the user to have a good amount of control without overwhelming. There are additional settings, options, etc. hidden behind a menu in the top right.



Project Brief and Functional Specifications

In the UX context, a project brief is a mandate for the project’s goals and what the expectations for the project are.

It’s meant to be a (brief) guide for: what the project is; why the project is; what the project requires/what the project’s barriers might be, etc.

Briefs can include whatever the designers deem relevant, pertinent, etc.; there isn’t an objective method to brief preparation (e.g. briefs can include research results, user personas, etc.)

Functional Specificiations

Functional specifications are what their name suggests: what the different facets of the project should do from a UX perspective (e.g. how does “search” function? Does it feature an autocomplete? Comparative Analysis

A comparative analysis represents a toe-to-toe (or close to toe-to-toe) comparison of features, advantages and disadvantages between products.

These analyses can inform designs. If your competitor’s product excels at a certain feature (e.g. good playlists, good search, etc.) then users will, for the most part, come to expect these features in each product within the same space. Failure to analyze – and, where possible, improve upon – what users’ appreciate about competitors’ designs will impede your product’s success.

What I Learned

I learned that it's difficult to design a system with priorities that (in some cases) almost compete; for example, premium content vs. user generated content. Where do you prioritize? How much do you mix the two, in terms of the results or filtered content that the user gets exposed to?

These are difficult information architecture questions; and I addressed them with as much time as I had (as it was a school project, there were very immediate deadlines to contend with, and then scary dreams for weeks afterwards that I'd missed those deadlines).

Throughout this project, I had excellent instructors, Matthew Humphreys (of T4G Kick) and Aaron Bernardo (of IBM) who guided me through the process and helped me to understand the nuances of UX Design, Information Architecture, and UX Research.

So: if you two are reading this: thank you for all of your support!
